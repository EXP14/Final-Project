Even if AI "thinks" differently from humans,
does that really mean artifical creativity is impossible?

In his paper "Developing Creativity: Artificial Barriers in Artificial Intelligence," Kyle Jennings argues that a system has creative autonomy if changes its standard "without explicit direction." Therefore, if sufficiently "smart" AI is embedded in a larger society of artists and audience where opinions can be learned and formed, there is possibility of creative autonomity.



Elkhova Oxana Igorevna and Kudryashev Alexandr Fedorovich from Bashkir State University argue that AI will eventually be able to compete with human creativity, but just not yet. In their paper "The Creative Ability of Artificial Intelligence," Igorevna and Fedorovich conclude that there are no "insurmountable obstacles to the competition between aritfical intelligence and man" and that the current limiting factor for AI creativity is the lack of a dataset great enough to compete with human common sense.

Looking in the present day, there is already room for AI art. The critic must not see AI as a human competitor, but as an instrument for humans to utilize. Then, a whole new medium arises where technology can gracefully meet with art.

In a blog post on Medium, David Vandegrift challenges the argument that AI can not be creative due to its inherent sole ability to follow the developer's instructions. Many argue that because AI only follows instructions, any resemblance of creativity should be accredited to the developer. But then, who is to say human creativity does not follow an instruction set from something beyond us?
